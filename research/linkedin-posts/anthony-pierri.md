## Post 1: Your Homepage Is Your Positioning Document

**Date:** March 2026
**Theme:** Homepage Messaging, Positioning

Hot take: Your homepage IS your positioning document.

Not a Google Doc. Not a Notion page. Not a 40-slide deck.

Your homepage.

If your homepage can't clearly explain:
→ WHO you're for
→ WHAT problem you solve
→ WHY you're different

...then your positioning isn't done.

I've reviewed 500+ SaaS homepages at FletchPMM.

The #1 mistake: trying to be everything to everyone.

"We're the all-in-one platform for modern teams."

What does that even mean?

Compare: "We help DevOps teams deploy to production 5x faster without breaking existing workflows."

One is vague. The other is a positioning statement that also works as ad copy, a LinkedIn headline, and a sales opener.

**Key Takeaway:** A homepage should function as the primary positioning document. If you can't explain who, what, and why in one clear homepage, your positioning needs work.

## Post 2: The Use-Case Anchor

**Date:** March 2026
**Theme:** Messaging Framework, Early-Stage SaaS

Early-stage SaaS founders: stop calling yourself a "platform."

Instead, anchor your messaging on a USE CASE.

A use case = the specific job your customer is doing when they need your product.

❌ "We're a customer success platform"
✅ "We help CS teams identify at-risk accounts before they churn"

❌ "We're a data integration tool"
✅ "We help data engineers move data from Salesforce to Snowflake in minutes"

Why use cases work:
1. They match how buyers actually search for solutions
2. They make your value immediately tangible
3. They differentiate you from broad competitors
4. They give your LinkedIn content a sharp angle

Write your next 10 LinkedIn posts about your use case. Watch engagement climb.

**Key Takeaway:** Anchoring messaging on a specific use case (rather than a broad category) makes LinkedIn content more relatable, searchable, and engaging for target buyers.

## Post 3: The Before/After Homepage Teardown

**Date:** February 2026
**Theme:** Content Format, Thought Leadership

The most popular content format I've ever created: the before/after homepage teardown.

Here's how it works:

1. I take a real SaaS homepage (with permission)
2. I show the BEFORE — usually vague, feature-focused, "platform" language
3. I show the AFTER — specific, use-case-anchored, buyer-focused messaging
4. I explain WHY each change matters

These posts consistently get 500+ likes and 100+ comments.

Why they work:
→ They're visual (people stop scrolling)
→ They're educational (people learn something)
→ They're specific (not generic "tips")
→ They're proof of work (I show my actual process)

If you're a consultant or agency, the "before/after" format is the single best LinkedIn content type for generating inbound leads.

**Key Takeaway:** Before/after teardowns are an extremely effective LinkedIn content format. They're visual, educational, specific, and serve as proof of expertise — driving high engagement and inbound leads.

## Post 4: Be Temporarily Narrow

**Date:** February 2026
**Theme:** Positioning Strategy, Go-to-Market

The advice I give EVERY early-stage SaaS founder:

"Be temporarily narrow."

Pick ONE segment. ONE use case. ONE ideal customer profile.

Focus your ENTIRE LinkedIn presence on that one thing for 3-4 months.

"But Anthony, what if we lose other opportunities?"

You won't. Here's why:

1. Narrow messaging attracts the exact right buyer
2. Other segments will find you anyway (if your product is good)
3. You can ALWAYS expand later
4. Trying to be broad means you resonate with nobody

The companies who are "temporarily narrow" on LinkedIn:
- Get more relevant engagement
- Close deals faster
- Build stronger brand association
- Waste less money on wrong-fit leads

You can always widen. You can't un-confuse your market.

**Key Takeaway:** "Be temporarily narrow" — focus LinkedIn content on one segment for 3-4 months. Narrow messaging resonates more strongly and can always be expanded later.

## Post 5: Champion-Centric Messaging

**Date:** January 2026
**Theme:** Buyer Personas, Messaging

Your LinkedIn content should target the CHAMPION, not the buyer.

Let me explain:

Champion = the person closest to the problem (usually mid-level)
Buyer = the person who signs the check (usually VP/C-level)

Most SaaS companies write content for the buyer:
"Reduce costs by 30%" — CFO language
"Increase revenue efficiency" — VP language

But the champion is the one who:
→ Finds your product
→ Advocates for it internally
→ Builds the business case
→ Champions the purchase

So your content should speak THEIR language:
"Spend 50% less time on manual data entry"
"Stop copying data between 5 different tools"
"Finally see which accounts are about to churn"

Champions drive deals. Write for champions.

**Key Takeaway:** LinkedIn content should be written for the "champion" (mid-level person closest to the problem), not the executive buyer. Champions find, advocate for, and drive purchasing decisions.
