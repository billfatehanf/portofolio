## Post 1: Founder-Led Marketing Is Not Optional

**Date:** March 2026
**Theme:** Founder Branding, B2B Strategy

If you're a B2B SaaS founder and you're not posting on LinkedIn, you're leaving money on the table.

I hear it all the time: "Dave, I'm not a marketer. I don't know what to post."

You don't need to be a marketer. You just need to share what you already know.

Here's what founders can post about:
- Why you started the company
- A customer problem you solved this week
- A mistake you made and what you learned
- An unpopular opinion about your industry
- The hiring decision you're wrestling with

That's it. No fancy strategy. Just be real.

The founders who do this consistently end up with:
→ Inbound leads from people who trust them
→ Easier recruiting (people want to work for real humans)
→ Press coverage (journalists follow LinkedIn too)
→ Investor interest (VCs are watching your brand)

Stop outsourcing your voice. Start posting today.

**Key Takeaway:** Founder-led marketing on LinkedIn is one of the highest-ROI activities for B2B SaaS companies. Authenticity and consistency beat polished corporate messaging.

## Post 2: The B2B Marketing Playbook is Broken

**Date:** March 2026
**Theme:** Industry Critique, Modern Marketing

The traditional B2B marketing playbook was written in 2010.

It looked like this:
1. Write an ebook
2. Gate it behind a form
3. Hand "leads" to sales
4. Run nurture sequences
5. Pray someone converts

In 2026, this playbook is completely broken.

Why? Because buyers changed:
- They Google before they fill out forms
- They ask peers in Slack/Discord communities
- They follow people on LinkedIn, not brands
- They make shortlists before talking to sales

The new playbook:
→ Create content that educates and builds trust
→ Be where your buyers are (LinkedIn, communities, podcasts)
→ Let buyers come to you when they're ready
→ Make it easy to buy (don't gate everything)

The old playbook optimized for "capturing demand." The new one optimizes for "creating demand."

**Key Takeaway:** The 2010 B2B playbook (gate content → capture leads → nurture) is dead. Modern B2B requires creating demand through ungated content, community presence, and personal brand.

## Post 4: The "Anti-Webinar" Philosophy

**Date:** February 2026
**Theme:** Content Format, B2B Events

Webinars are dying. Here's what's replacing them.

The average B2B webinar:
- 45 min of slides nobody wants to see
- A "Q&A" where 3 people ask questions
- A recording that nobody watches
- 500 "registrants" and 50 who actually show up

At Exit Five, we replaced webinars with:
→ 20-min live sessions with ONE expert
→ No slides, just conversation
→ Real Q&A where the host drives questions
→ Cut into 60-second clips for LinkedIn
→ Posted natively (not as a link to a recording)

Result: 3x more engagement, 5x more comments, and people actually remember what was said.

Don't create more webinars. Create better conversations. Then repurpose them.

**Key Takeaway:** Short, conversational live sessions outperform traditional webinars for B2B. The key is to make them repurposable — cut into LinkedIn-native clips for organic distribution.

## Post 5: People Follow People

**Date:** January 2026
**Theme:** Personal Brand, LinkedIn Strategy

A phrase I've been repeating for 5 years: "People follow people, not brands."

I used to say this when I was CMO at Drift. My personal LinkedIn got more engagement than Drift's company page — with 1/100th of the followers.

Why?
- People want to learn from other people
- They want to see the human behind the company
- They trust personal experiences over corporate messaging
- LinkedIn's algorithm favors personal profiles

This is why the smartest B2B companies are investing in:
1. Executive content programs
2. Employee advocacy (real, not forced reshares)
3. Creator partnerships with industry voices
4. Founder storytelling

If your CEO isn't on LinkedIn, you're fighting with one hand tied behind your back.

**Key Takeaway:** "People follow people, not brands" is the foundational principle of B2B LinkedIn strategy. Companies must invest in personal brands of their executives and employees, not just the corporate page.
