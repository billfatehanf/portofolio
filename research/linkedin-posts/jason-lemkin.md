## Post 1: AI Is Rewriting Every SaaS Playbook

**Date:** March 2026
**Theme:** AI in SaaS, Scaling

The playbooks that got you to $10M ARR in 2022 won't get you to $20M in 2026.

Why? AI changed everything:

- Your SDR team can be 80% smaller (AI handles the first touch)
- Your content team can produce 10x more with AI drafts + human editing
- Your support team can deflect 60% of tickets automatically
- Your product team ships features in days, not months

But here's what AI can NOT replace:
→ Genuine thought leadership on LinkedIn
→ Trust built through authentic content
→ Relationships formed in communities
→ The CEO who posts their real opinions

AI makes the efficient stuff cheaper. It makes the human stuff MORE valuable.

If you're a SaaS founder, your LinkedIn presence is now your #1 moat.

**Key Takeaway:** AI commoditizes execution but amplifies the value of human trust and thought leadership. LinkedIn organic content is becoming more valuable, not less, in the AI era.

## Post 2: The Rule of 40 is Dead. Long Live the Rule of Efficiency.

**Date:** February 2026
**Theme:** SaaS Metrics, Growth Strategy

For years, SaaS investors lived by the Rule of 40.

(Growth rate + profit margin ≥ 40% = good)

In 2026, the Rule of 40 isn't enough.

What investors and boards care about now:
1. Net Revenue Retention (NRR) — are customers expanding?
2. CAC Payback — how fast do you earn back customer acquisition cost?
3. Pipeline Source Mix — what % comes from organic/brand vs paid?

That last one is critical.

The best SaaS companies I work with at SaaStr have 40-60% of pipeline sourced from organic channels (LinkedIn, community, word-of-mouth, podcast).

This isn't because organic is "free." It's because it's TRUSTED.

Buyers who come in through organic content convert faster, retain longer, and expand more.

Invest in organic. It's the new competitive moat.

**Key Takeaway:** Top SaaS companies source 40-60% of pipeline from organic channels. Organic is not just cheaper — it produces higher-quality pipeline that converts and retains better.

## Post 3: What Founders Get Wrong About LinkedIn

**Date:** February 2026
**Theme:** Founder Content, LinkedIn Strategy

I've mentored 1,000+ SaaS founders through SaaStr.

The #1 LinkedIn mistake they make: treating it like a press release channel.

"We're excited to announce our Series B!"
"Thrilled to share that we've been named a Leader in Gartner's..."
"We're hiring! Check out our open roles..."

Nobody engages with these posts. Nobody.

What works:
→ "We almost ran out of money twice before finding PMF. Here's what I learned."
→ "My co-founder and I disagreed about pricing for 6 months. Here's who was right."
→ "I fired our top salesperson. It was the hardest decision I've made this year."

Vulnerability. Specificity. Lessons.

That's what builds a founder brand on LinkedIn.

**Key Takeaway:** Press-release-style LinkedIn posts get zero engagement. Founders who share vulnerable, specific stories with real lessons build genuine followings that drive business outcomes.

## Post 4: The SaaStr Community Effect

**Date:** January 2026
**Theme:** Community, Content Distribution

SaaStr started as a blog in 2012.

Today it's:
- 100M+ annual content views
- 15,000 attendees at our annual event
- 500k+ social followers across platforms
- The largest B2B SaaS community in the world

How did we get here? Not through paid ads.

Through 14 years of consistent, ungated, opinionated content.

My formula:
1. Post 2-3x per day on LinkedIn (yes, daily)
2. Answer real founder questions publicly
3. Share specific numbers, not vague platitudes
4. Take positions that challenge conventional wisdom
5. Never gate anything behind a form

The compound effect of organic content is staggering. But you have to commit to YEARS, not months.

**Key Takeaway:** SaaStr's massive brand was built entirely through consistent, ungated, opinionated organic content over 14 years. The compound effect of daily LinkedIn posting is powerful but requires multi-year commitment.

## Post 5: Your VP of Marketing Should Be on LinkedIn

**Date:** January 2026
**Theme:** Executive Branding, Marketing Leadership

If your VP of Marketing isn't actively posting on LinkedIn, they're missing a massive opportunity.

I'm not saying they need to be an "influencer."

I AM saying:
- Their perspective shapes how the market sees your category
- Their posts attract top talent who want to work for smart leaders
- Their content generates inbound pipeline (yes, from LinkedIn posts)
- Their presence builds the company brand through personal brand

The best VPs of Marketing I know post 3-5x per week.

They share: hot takes on their category, lessons from campaigns, hiring philosophies, and honest assessments of what's working.

It's not a "nice-to-have" anymore. It's part of the job.

**Key Takeaway:** VP-level marketing leaders should be posting 3-5x per week on LinkedIn. Their personal brand directly impacts recruiting, pipeline, and category perception.
