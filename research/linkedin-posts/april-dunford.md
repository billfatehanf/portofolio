## Post 1: Positioning Is Not Messaging

**Date:** March 2026
**Theme:** Positioning, B2B Marketing Fundamentals

I need to say this louder for the people in the back:

Positioning is NOT messaging.

Positioning = The strategic context you set for your product
Messaging = The words you use to communicate that context

You can't write good messaging without positioning.
You CAN have good positioning and bad messaging.

Yet every week I get DMs saying: "April, can you help us with our messaging?"

When I dig in, the problem is never the words. It's always the strategy underneath.

They haven't defined:
→ Who is the ideal customer?
→ What competitive alternatives exist?
→ What unique capabilities do they have?
→ What is the value those capabilities deliver?
→ What market category frames the value best?

Fix positioning first. Messaging becomes obvious.

**Key Takeaway:** Most messaging problems are actually positioning problems. Before writing LinkedIn content or website copy, companies must first define their strategic positioning using a structured framework.

## Post 2: The "Obviously Awesome" Framework in Practice

**Date:** March 2026
**Theme:** Positioning Framework, SaaS Strategy

I've run 200+ positioning workshops with B2B SaaS companies.

Here are the 5 components of positioning (in order):

1. **Competitive alternatives** — What would customers use if you didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes** — What do you have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value** — What benefit do those attributes deliver?
4. **Target customer** — Who cares MOST about that value?
5. **Market category** — What context makes the value obvious?

Most companies start at the end: "We're an AI platform for..."

Wrong. Start with #1. Everything flows from there.

When you post on LinkedIn, test your positioning. If your posts explaining your product get confusion, your positioning needs work.

LinkedIn is the best free positioning testing lab in the world.

**Key Takeaway:** The 5-component positioning framework (competitive alternatives → unique attributes → value → target customer → market category) should inform all LinkedIn content. LinkedIn itself is a free testing ground for positioning.

## Post 3: Stop Calling Yourself a "Platform"

**Date:** February 2026
**Theme:** Positioning, Messaging Mistakes

The most overused word in B2B SaaS: "Platform."

"We're a revenue intelligence platform."
"We're an employee engagement platform."
"We're a data unification platform."

What does "platform" even mean?

To customers, it means: "We do a bunch of stuff, but we can't explain what specifically."

Early-stage companies: you are NOT a platform.

You solve ONE specific problem for ONE specific buyer better than anything else.

Say THAT on your LinkedIn. Say THAT on your homepage.

"We help DevOps teams reduce deployment failures by 50%."
"We help CFOs close the books 5 days faster."
"We help sales managers see exactly why deals stall."

Specificity builds trust. "Platform" builds confusion.

**Key Takeaway:** Vague positioning ("platform") kills B2B content. Specificity about the problem you solve and for whom makes LinkedIn posts resonate and builds trust.

## Post 4: Why Your Sales Narrative Doesn't Work

**Date:** February 2026
**Theme:** Sales Narrative, Positioning

Most B2B SaaS sales pitches follow this structure:

"Here's our company. Here's our product. Here are the features."

This is backwards.

The best sales narratives follow this structure:

1. **The insight:** What's changed in the world that creates urgency?
2. **The problem:** What are the consequences of not adapting?
3. **The solution criteria:** What should a good solution look like?
4. **The proof:** Here's how we uniquely deliver this.

Notice: your product doesn't show up until step 4.

This same framework works on LinkedIn:
→ Start with the insight (hook)
→ Describe the problem (build tension)
→ Hint at the solution criteria (build authority)
→ Position yourself as the answer (soft CTA)

Positioning → sales narrative → LinkedIn content. It's all connected.

**Key Takeaway:** The sales narrative framework (insight → problem → solution criteria → proof) maps directly to high-performing LinkedIn posts. Leading with change and tension is more effective than leading with your product.

## Post 5: The LinkedIn Content Test for Positioning

**Date:** January 2026
**Theme:** Content Strategy, Positioning Validation

Here's a secret: I use LinkedIn as my positioning research lab.

When I'm helping a client figure out their positioning, I post about their problem space.

I test different angles:
- "The #1 reason DevOps teams fail at CI/CD..."
- "Why CFOs are drowning in manual close processes..."
- "The hidden cost of bad data quality in RevOps..."

The post that gets the most engagement from the RIGHT audience tells me which problem resonates most.

Then I look at the comments:
- What language do people use to describe their pain?
- What competitive alternatives do they mention?
- What outcomes do they care about most?

This is FREE market research.

If you're a B2B SaaS founder, use your LinkedIn content as a positioning hypothesis test. Every post is data.

**Key Takeaway:** LinkedIn posts can serve as free market research for testing positioning hypotheses. The engagement patterns and comment language reveal what resonates with your target audience.
