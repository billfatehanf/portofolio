## Video 1: "How to Position Your B2B SaaS Product (Full Workshop)"

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **URL** | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aprildunford-positioning-workshop |
| **Date** | September 2025 |
| **Duration** | ~52 min |
| **Speaker** | April Dunford |

### Transcript Summary

April walks through her 5-step positioning framework in detail:

"Positioning is the fundamental strategic exercise that determines everything downstream. Your messaging, your content, your sales pitch, your LinkedIn posts — they all come from positioning. If your positioning is weak, nothing else matters."

**Step 1 — Competitive Alternatives:**
"The first question isn't 'how do we describe our product.' The first question is: 'What would customers do if we didn't exist?' Maybe they'd use a competitor. Maybe they'd use a spreadsheet. Maybe they'd hire an intern. Understanding the competitive alternative tells you what you're really competing against — and it's often not who you think."

"On LinkedIn, this translates to: don't talk about your product. Talk about the PROBLEM and the crappy solutions people are currently using. That's what resonates."

**Step 2 — Unique Attributes:**
"Once you know the alternative, list everything your product does that the alternative cannot. Be specific. Not 'better UX' — that's vague. Something like: 'automated data reconciliation that catches errors in real-time.' Specificity is everything."

**Step 3 — Value:**
"Now translate those attributes into value. What does the customer GET from those unique capabilities? Not features. Outcomes. 'Saves 10 hours per week.' 'Reduces errors by 90%.' 'Closes the books 5 days faster.'"

"Your LinkedIn posts should be about the VALUE, not the features. Talk about outcomes. Outcomes resonate. Features bore people."

**Step 4 — Target Customer:**
"Who cares MOST about the value you deliver? This is your best-fit customer. Not everyone. Not 'all companies.' The specific type of company, the specific role, the specific situation where your value is most acute."

"On LinkedIn, this means: write for your target customer. If they're DevOps managers at mid-market companies, your content should speak directly to DevOps challenges at mid-market companies. Not the Fortune 500. Not startups. Mid-market."

**Step 5 — Market Category:**
"Finally: what context makes all of this obvious? If I say 'email marketing platform,' you immediately understand roughly what it does, who it's for, and what alternatives exist. The category is the shorthand that frames everything."

"The trap: early-stage founders try to create a new category. 'We're the first Intelligent Revenue Orchestration Platform.' Nobody knows what that means. Pick a category your buyer already understands, and differentiate within it."

### Key Themes
- 5-step positioning framework: Competitive alternatives → Unique attributes → Value → Target customer → Market category
- LinkedIn content should focus on problems and outcomes, not features
- Write for your specific target customer, not everyone
- Avoid creating new categories early; differentiate within existing ones
- Positioning is the foundation; all content flows from it

## Video 2: "Sales Narratives That Actually Close Deals"

| **Duration** | ~40 min |

### Transcript Summary

April presents her "sales narrative" framework, extending positioning into storytelling:

"The problem with most sales decks is they start with 'About Us.' Nobody cares about you. Start with what's changed in the MARKET that makes your solution relevant NOW."

**The Narrative Arc:**
"Here's the structure that works:

1. THE INSIGHT: Something has fundamentally changed. A shift in the market, a new regulation, a technology breakthrough. This creates urgency. 'In 2025, AI search changed how buyers discover B2B software. Companies that relied purely on SEO saw traffic drop 40%.'

2. THE PROBLEM: Because of this change, something is broken for your buyer. 'Most B2B marketing teams are still optimizing for Google keywords that are losing volume every quarter.'

3. THE CRITERIA: Here's what a good solution to this problem should look like. Not YOUR solution yet. The general characteristics. 'A modern B2B content strategy must prioritize channels the company OWNS — like LinkedIn — where they can build direct trust.'

4. THE PROOF: Here's how WE specifically deliver this. Now you introduce your product, positioned perfectly because the narrative built up to it."

**Applying the narrative to LinkedIn:**
"This is exactly how the best LinkedIn posts work. The top-performing LinkedIn content I've seen follows this exact arc:

Hook = The Insight (something has changed)
Body = The Problem + Criteria (what's broken and what a fix looks like)
CTA = The Proof (soft mention of how the author/company addresses it)

If every LinkedIn post you write follows: Insight → Problem → Criteria → Proof, your content will stand out from 99% of what's out there."

### Key Themes
- Sales narratives should start with market insight, not product description
- The 4-part narrative: Insight → Problem → Criteria → Proof
- This same framework maps directly to high-performing LinkedIn content
- Lead with change and urgency, not features
